# 🎭 Player's Guide to Relay RPG
*Theory, craft, and tips for getting the most out of collaborative play*

---

## What Kind of Game Is This?

Relay RPG is a **play-by-post storytelling game**. You're not trying to win — you're trying to tell a story worth reading. The dice create stakes, not outcomes. What the dice give you is a *constraint*, and constraints are what make stories interesting.

Think of it less like a board game and more like **collaborative improv with consequences**.

---

## What a Turn Looks Like

A turn has three parts — all written together as the description you type into `/turn`:

**1. Interpret the previous roll**
Open by acknowledging what the last result meant for the scene. Don't skip this — it's the connective tissue of the story.

**2. Narrate your character's action**
Describe what your character does in response. Be specific. End on something with genuine stakes — a moment where success and failure would each pull the story somewhere different.

**3. Roll**
`/turn` sends your narration and rolls the dice. The result shapes what the next player inherits.

### A Full Example

*Scene: You're trying to break into a guarded facility. The previous player rolled a **7 (Partial)**.*

> **Player B types into `/turn`:**
> 
> *"The window catch gives — but the frame scrapes loud against the sill. Somewhere below, a guard stops walking. I freeze, pressed flat against the wall, watching the beam of his flashlight sweep closer. I have maybe three seconds to get through and pull the frame shut before the light finds me."*
> 
> → rolls **9 (Success)**

Player B did three things: acknowledged the partial (the noise), put themselves in a tense situation, and ended on a clear action with stakes. The dice said it succeeded — so Player C opens their turn by writing what that success *looks like*, then declares their own action and rolls.

## The Core Skill: Building on What Exists

The single most important thing you can do as a player is **make what came before you more interesting** — not replace it.

When the previous player rolled a failure and said *"the lock won't budge"*, your job isn't to ignore that and pick a different lock. Your job is to live in that world where the lock won't budge and find what's interesting there.

### The "Yes, And" → "Yes, But" Spectrum

| Roll | Connector | What it means |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| ⭐ Critical Success | "Yes, and then some—" | Their action worked brilliantly AND opened a new door |
| ✅ Success | "And then—" | Build forward, add something |
| 🤔 Partial | "...but—" | It sort of worked, with a cost or complication |
| ❌ Failure | "No, and—" | It didn't work, AND now something is worse |
| 💥 Critical Failure | "No, and it gets much worse—" | A genuine escalation |

> **The partial is the most common result statistically.** Learning to write *interesting complications* is more valuable than learning to write victories.

## Reading the Dice Like a Storyteller

### What Each Result Asks You to Do

**⭐ Critical Success (11–12)**
Something went *spectacularly* right. Don't just succeed — exceed. Open a door no one expected. Introduce a gift that will matter later.
> *"Not only does the mechanism click open — you find a hidden compartment inside it with a message addressed to one of you."*

**✅ Success (9–10)**
Clean forward motion. Your action worked. Carry momentum — don't stall here, hand the scene something to work with.
> *"The signal goes through. Someone picks up."*

**🤔 Partial (6–8)**
The richest result to write. Something worked, but with a cost, complication, or uncomfortable truth. The best partials:
- Introduce a new problem that's different from the old one
- Put the next player in an interesting position
- Reveal something about the situation or a character

> *"The door opens — but not before the chain snaps and the noise echoes down the hall."*

**❌ Failure (4–5)**
Don't just say "it didn't work." Show what that failure *means*. What does the world do in response? Failures push the story sideways.
> *"The guard recognizes you. He's not raising the alarm yet — but his hand is on the radio."*

**💥 Critical Failure (2–3)**
Things got worse in a way that stings. But even a critical failure is a story gift — the best moments in any story come from things going spectacularly wrong.
> *"The device overloads and sends a feedback pulse through every system in the building. Lights die. Alarms wake."*

## Tension — The Partial Spiral

**Partials build tension** (shown as 🔥 in your roll results). Three in a row triggers an automatic failure.

This isn't a punishment — it's a pacing mechanism. If your table keeps landing partials, the story is building toward something. The tension bar is the narrative pressure gauge.

**When tension is rising, lean into it.** Your narration should reflect that things feel increasingly precarious, even if nothing has technically broken yet.

> 🔥 Tension 1/3: *"You almost have it..."*  
> 🔥🔥 Tension 2/3: *"Your hands are shaking. Something feels wrong."*  
> 💢 Tension Breaks: *"That's when everything slips."*

## The Transition — Your Biggest Creative Moment

When an Act concludes (3 successes or 3 failures), the completing player writes the **transition** to the next scene. This is often the most impactful writing in the whole session.

### The Formula: **"[Result], but [complication], therefore [next setup]"**

The key words are **but** and **therefore**. These create *causality* — each scene feels like it grows from the last.

Avoid: *"We succeeded. Now we go somewhere else."* (No causal link)  
Prefer: *"We got the information, but we burned our only contact to get it, therefore now we're on our own heading into the compound."*

### Success vs. Failure Transitions

A **success transition** shouldn't feel like the story is over — victories come with complications. What did winning *cost*, and what does it open?

A **failure transition** shouldn't feel like a dead end — failures redirect, they don't stop. What did going wrong *reveal*, and how does that change the next move?

> The best transitions make the next scene feel *inevitable* given what just happened.

## Character Traits — Using Them Well

You get **one use each** of your Conviction, Talent, and Quirk per adventure — once spent, they're gone until the next story. That scarcity matters.

### Don't burn traits early just to boost a roll you'd probably make anyway.

The +2 bonus shifts your odds meaningfully, but traits have a second function: **they're narrative declarations**. When you invoke a trait, you're saying *this moment is important enough to define my character by.*

### Three good times to use a trait:
1. **When failure would end something important** — a scene teetering on 2 failures, a critical moment
2. **When you want to make a moment land** — the climax of an arc you've been building personally
3. **When the trait fits the fiction perfectly** — it would feel wrong *not* to use it here

### Three times to hold off:
1. The first roll of the game (you haven't earned the dramatic weight yet)
2. When a partial is honestly fine — complications drive good stories
3. When someone else's trait use would tell the better story right now

## Scene Truths — Worldbuilding Is Power

At the start of each Act, every player can declare **one Truth** about the scene. Truths become canon instantly.

**Good truths do two things at once:**
- They establish something useful or interesting about the environment
- They subtly set up something for later

> *"The station has been abandoned for at least a decade — the emergency lights are running on backup power and they're almost out."*  
> (Sets atmosphere AND gives future rolls a ticking clock)

**Truths can be about:**
- Physical details of the location
- Facts about the situation or history
- Things your character knows or has noticed
- Emotional or social dynamics in the room

Truths are not actions — you're not saying what your character does, you're saying what *is*. Use them to set up the world you want to play in.

## Playing Your Character — Not Yourself

The game works best when players are **genuinely curious about their characters**, not just projecting a power fantasy.

Your character has a **Conviction** (what they believe or protect), a **Talent** (what they're actually good at), and a **Quirk** (what makes them interesting/complicated). The richest play happens when these three things are in *tension* with each other.

> A character with **Conviction:** *"Never leave anyone behind"*, **Talent:** *"Infiltration and stealth"*, and **Quirk:** *"Terrible at lying"* will face genuinely interesting decisions. Their stealth training says *slip away*, their conviction says *go back*, and their honesty will make any cover story a disaster.

**Ask yourself before each turn:**  
*What does my character actually want here, and what are they afraid of?*

## The Host's Role

If you're the **Host** (the player who created the story with `/post`):

- You set the opening scene with `/begin` — make it atmospheric, make it *immediate*. Start in the middle of something happening, not before it.
- You write the finale with `/finale` — honor what the dice and players built. Don't override the story they made; reflect it back with weight.
- During play, you're a participant like everyone else — but you hold the connective tissue of the world.
- You can `/invite` other players mid-story, and you're the only one who can `/remove` someone.

**A good opening scene answers three questions:**
1. Where are we?
2. What is happening right now?
3. What decision do the players face immediately?

---

## Epilogues — Closing With Intention

After the finale, everyone posts an epilogue. This is your character's final word in the story. Pick whichever type feels true:

**📖 Character Growth** — What changed for them? What did they learn or lose or become?  
**🧵 Unresolved Thread** — What question does this story leave unanswered? What remains?  
**🔮 Future Hook** — What seed did this story plant? What might grow from it?

> A good epilogue doesn't summarize what happened — it reveals what it *meant*.

## Quick Mechanics Reference

| Mechanic | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Dice | 2d6 |
| Critical Failure | ≤3 → +2 failures (capped at 3) |
| Failure | 4–5 → +1 failure |
| Partial | 6–8 → builds tension (no direct effect) |
| Success | 9–10 → +1 success |
| Critical Success | ≥11 → +2 successes (capped at 3) |
| Scene ends | 3 successes or 3 failures |
| Tension | 3 consecutive partials = +1 failure (auto) |
| Traits | +2 bonus, each used once per story |
| Trait use | Must be narrated into the action |
| Acts | 4 total (Act I, II, III, Climax) |
| Failed acts | Carry into Climax as starting failures |

## Common Mistakes (and What to Do Instead)

**❌ Writing a turn that ignores the previous result**  
✅ Start your turn by acknowledging or building on what just happened

**❌ Ending your turn with a safe action where success and failure feel identical**  
✅ End with something that has genuine risk — where the dice *matter* because both outcomes would change the story differently

**❌ Using a trait on the first roll "just in case"**  
✅ Wait for a moment that genuinely calls for it

**❌ Transition: "We succeeded. We go to the next place."**  
✅ Transition: "We succeeded, *but* it cost us X, *therefore* now we face Y"

**❌ Truth: "There's a sword on the table."** (No story function)  
✅ Truth: "The sword on the table has a name carved into the blade — someone's name you recognize."

**❌ Starting your turn by saying "You fail, nothing happens"**  
✅ Building on the failure — what did it cost, reveal, or set in motion?

**❌ Playing a character who is good at everything**  
✅ Playing a character whose strengths and flaws pull in different directions

---

*The best play-by-post games feel like reading a novel that no one planned but everyone made together. Bring your curiosity, build on what's there, and trust the dice to make it interesting.*
